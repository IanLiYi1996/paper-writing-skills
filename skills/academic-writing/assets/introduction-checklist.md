# Introduction Section Checklist

Use this checklist while drafting and revising your Introduction. For detailed guidance and phrases, see [introduction-guide.md](../references/introduction-guide.md).

---

## Pre-Writing

- [ ] Identified 2-4 specific contributions to highlight
- [ ] Listed 5-10 key related papers to cite in the Introduction
- [ ] Clarified the specific research gap your work addresses
- [ ] Determined target audience (specialists vs. broader community)
- [ ] Formulated one-sentence contribution statement

---

## Stage 1: Opening Context

- [ ] Opened with compelling, specific statement (not "In recent years..." or "With the rapid development of...")
- [ ] Established broad importance of research area
- [ ] Used concrete examples, statistics, or real-world applications
- [ ] Connected to problems readers care about
- [ ] Engaged both specialist and non-specialist readers
- [ ] Avoided unnecessary jargon in opening paragraph

---

## Stage 2: Prior Work and Current State

- [ ] Cited 5-10 key related papers (comprehensive coverage goes in Related Work)
- [ ] Grouped approaches thematically, not chronologically
- [ ] Acknowledged strengths of existing work fairly
- [ ] Identified patterns or commonalities across approaches
- [ ] Maintained professional, respectful tone toward prior work
- [ ] Set up natural transition to limitations

---

## Stage 3: Research Gap

- [ ] Specified clear, concrete limitations of existing work
- [ ] Used diplomatic hedging ("less attention has been paid to," "remains challenging")
- [ ] Explained why the gap matters (connected to real problem or need)
- [ ] Avoided dismissive language ("fails to," "ignores," "unfortunately")
- [ ] Gap naturally motivates your proposed approach
- [ ] Quantified scope of the problem when possible

---

## Stage 4: Proposed Approach

- [ ] Provided high-level description (coarse-grained first)
- [ ] Explained key insight or intuition behind approach
- [ ] Highlighted what makes approach different from prior work
- [ ] Limited to 1-2 paragraphs
- [ ] Saved detailed technical specifications for Methods
- [ ] Explained "why this should work" not just "what you do"
- [ ] Approach sounds motivated and principled (not arbitrary)

---

## Stage 5: Contributions

- [ ] Listed 2-4 specific contributions (not more, not fewer)
- [ ] Used bullet points for clarity
- [ ] Each contribution is concrete and verifiable
- [ ] Ordered by importance or logical flow
- [ ] Distinguished contribution types (method, finding, resource, analysis)
- [ ] Each contribution is independently valuable
- [ ] Used specific language: "We propose X which achieves Y" not "We propose a new method"
- [ ] Contributions match what is validated in Results/Conclusion
- [ ] Bullets are 1-2 lines each in two-column format

---

## Overall Structure and Flow

- [ ] Follows clear funnel: broad context -> prior work -> gap -> approach -> contributions
- [ ] Smooth transitions between stages
- [ ] Each paragraph has clear focus and purpose
- [ ] Logic flows naturally from motivation to contribution
- [ ] Introduction is self-contained
- [ ] Length is appropriate (1-1.5 pages for conferences)
- [ ] No technical details that belong in Methods
- [ ] Methods section begins by page 2-3

---

## Language and Style

- [ ] Strong opening hook (not generic or weak)
- [ ] Active voice where appropriate ("We propose..." not "A method is proposed...")
- [ ] Varied sentence structure
- [ ] Appropriate hedging (not "always" or "never")
- [ ] No contractions
- [ ] Minimal vague terms ("very," "quite," "some")
- [ ] Consistent terminology throughout
- [ ] Confident but not overselling

---

## Citations

- [ ] 5-10 key papers cited in Introduction
- [ ] Citations are recent and relevant
- [ ] Correct citation format (citet for in-text, citep for parenthetical)
- [ ] Prior work acknowledged fairly
- [ ] No orphan citations (cited but not discussed)
- [ ] Citations support the claims being made

---

## Consistency with Other Sections

- [ ] Contributions match those validated in Conclusion
- [ ] Abstract and Introduction tell consistent story
- [ ] Research gap is addressed by your work
- [ ] Terminology introduced here is used consistently throughout
- [ ] Methods section delivers on promises made here

---

## Quick Self-Assessment

1. **Hook**: Does the opening make someone want to keep reading?
2. **Clarity**: Can you explain your contribution in one sentence?
3. **Gap**: Is it crystal clear what is missing in prior work?
4. **Specificity**: Are contributions concrete enough that a skeptic could verify them?
5. **Flow**: Does each paragraph naturally lead to the next?
6. **Motivation**: Would a reviewer think this problem is worth solving?
